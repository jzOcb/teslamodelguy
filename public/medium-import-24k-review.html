Tesla Model Y Juniper: What It Actually Costs After 24,000 Miles

Everyone talks about the sticker price. Nobody talks about what happens after. After 24,000 miles of daily driving a 2026 Model Y Juniper Long Range, we tracked every cost — electricity, maintenance, tires, charging on the road — and the numbers might change how you think about buying a car.

Last updated: March 2026 · 10 min read

💰 Quick Summary

After 24,000 miles: total running costs of ~$1,775. Maintenance: $0 (just washer fluid and tire rotations). Electricity: 4.25¢/mile vs 12.5¢/mile for a comparable gas SUV. 5-year projected savings: $6,500–$7,000 vs a gas SUV.

Electricity: What You're Actually Paying Per Mile

The Model Y Juniper Long Range averages 250 Wh per mile in real-world driving — not the EPA estimate, actual driving over 24,000 miles including highway, city, winter, and summer.

That works out to about 4 miles per kWh. At the national average electricity rate of $0.17/kWh, your cost is 4.25 cents per mile.

Here's what makes that number impressive: the Model Y Juniper is 850 lbs heavier than a Model 3 and gets the exact same efficiency. Tesla's engineering improvements have completely offset the extra weight.

Home Charging vs Supercharging

About 90% of charging happens at home. Plug in when you get home, charge to 80% overnight, done. Most days you won't use more than 30% of the battery.

  • Home (overnight): ~$6-7 per full charge at $0.17/kWh
  • Supercharger (road trip): ~$12-15 for 10% → 60% (~150 miles)
  • Free charging: Hotels, malls, workplaces — more common than you'd think

The Juniper charges from 10% → 60% in just 18 minutes, and 10% → 90% in about 35 minutes.

Maintenance After 24,000 Miles: The $0 Reality

This is where the cost-of-ownership math gets wild. After 24,000 miles, here's the complete maintenance ledger:

  • Windshield washer fluid: ~$15/year — the car uses more than you'd expect (auto-cleans FSD cameras)
  • Tire rotations every 6,250 miles: $0 (DIY) or ~$80/year
  • Oil changes: $0 — no engine, no oil
  • Brake pads & rotors: $0 — regenerative braking does 100% of the work
  • Service visits / recalls: $0 — zero trips to Tesla in 24,000 miles

A comparable gas SUV would have needed 2-3 oil changes ($70-100 each), cabin air filter, and would be 24,000 miles closer to needing brake work. That's $300-500 in maintenance costs that simply don't exist.

Brakes: Why You May Never Replace Them

This is the single most underrated cost advantage of EVs. The Model Y uses regenerative braking for virtually 100% of stopping. The traditional hydraulic disc brakes barely engage.

A Model 3 with 90,000 miles still has original brake pads that look brand new. That's not an exaggeration. On a gas SUV, brake pads typically need replacement every 30,000-70,000 miles ($250-500 per axle). On a Tesla, you might never replace them during normal ownership.

Tires: Real-World Lifespan

With rotations every 6,250 miles, the original tires are on track for about 50,000 miles. Watch for slightly more wear on outer edges due to the Model Y's higher center of gravity and battery weight. Keep them inflated to 42 PSI and rotate on schedule.

💡 A set of replacement tires runs $800-1,200. At 50,000 miles per set, that's about 2¢/mile — still cheaper than gas per mile.

The Hidden Costs Nobody Mentions

  • Washer Fluid: ~$15-20/year — auto-spray for FSD cameras uses 3-4x more than normal
  • Perforated Seats: Ventilated seat holes trap crumbs and are harder to clean
  • Occasional Interior Rattles: A faint rattle from the rear seat area, ~5-10% of drives
  • Uneven Tire Wear: Heavier weight causes slightly more wear on tire edges

Model Y vs Gas SUV: 5-Year Cost Projection

Based on 12,000 miles/year:

  • Fuel/Electricity: Model Y $2,550 vs Gas SUV $7,500
  • Oil Changes: Model Y $0 vs Gas SUV $750
  • Brake Work: Model Y $0 vs Gas SUV $500
  • Tire Rotations: Model Y $0-400 vs Gas SUV $400
  • Other Maintenance: Model Y $0 vs Gas SUV $500
  • Tires (1 replacement): Model Y $1,000 vs Gas SUV $800
  • Total (5 years): Model Y $3,550-$3,950 vs Gas SUV $10,450

That's roughly $6,500-$7,000 in savings over 5 years on running costs alone — before considering the $7,500 federal tax credit.

Is It Worth It?

After 24,000 miles, the Model Y has needed nothing but windshield washer fluid and tire rotations. No oil changes, no brake work, no dealer visits, no recalls. The car is mechanically identical to the day it was delivered.

If you drive 12,000+ miles a year and have home charging, the math is straightforward. The Model Y Juniper isn't just cheaper to fuel — it's cheaper to own, period.
